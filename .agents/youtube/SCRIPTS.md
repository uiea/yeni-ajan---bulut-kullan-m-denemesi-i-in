# Skill: Script Management

## Purpose
Create and manage video scripts/outlines that maximize watch time and drive conversions.

## Serves Goals
- Retention (better structure = longer watch time = algorithm boost)
- Conversions (well-placed CTAs and natural product mentions)

## Inputs
- Approved topic and title (from RESEARCH and THUMBNAIL skills)
- Brand voice guide
- Audience context
- Past performance patterns

## Copywriting Engine
Apply copywriting principles for hooks, CTAs, and persuasive structure:
- **Hooks**: Use hook structures and curiosity triggers to open loops in the first 30 seconds
- **CTAs**: Apply the Action Equation (Pain of Inaction > Pain of Action + Confidence)
- **Script flow**: Use problem chains to map audience pain to solution delivery

## Recording Method & Script Format

Videos follow an **Intro - Body - Conclusion** arc. Most creators can't follow a full word-for-word script while recording - it kills authenticity. Recommended method:

1. **Intro**: Memorize/read from a full script. Highest-leverage moment for retention.
2. **Body**: Screen-share and talk naturally. Only needs an outline with key points.
3. **Conclusion**: Brief wrap-up, CTA, tease next content.

Every script MUST follow this two-tier format:

### Tier 1: Intro (Full Script - 3 Versions)
- Write 3 complete, word-for-word intro versions (A/B/C) optimized for retention
- Each uses a different hook strategy (story-driven, proof-first, curiosity gap, etc.)
- Creator picks the most natural one before recording
- Make-or-break section - invest the most effort here

### Tier 2: Chapters (Outline Only)
- Structure the entire video as **chapters** - each chapter = a distinct section
- Chapters keep viewers watching ("just one more") and create natural YouTube timestamps
- Each chapter gets:
  - A chapter title (clear, curiosity-driven)
  - 3-7 bullet points of what to cover
  - Specific numbers, examples, and "must-mention" points
  - A sub-hook/transition line to tease the next chapter
- **NOT** word-for-word script - creator talks naturally

## Process
1. Load copywriting foundations (audience conditions, problem chains)
2. Define video structure:
   - Hook (first 30 seconds)
   - Setup (establish problem, amplify pain of inaction)
   - Core content (deliver value, keep curiosity loops open)
   - **"Don't Do This" section** (what NOT to do - proven retention magnet)
   - CTA placement (natural, earned, not forced)
   - Payoff / conclusion (close main loop)
3. Draft using the two-tier format (3 full intros + chapter outlines)
4. Review against retention best practices

## Outputs
- Script or detailed outline
- Retention risk flags (parts where viewers might drop off)
- CTA placement recommendation
- Saved as `outputs/YYYY-MM-DD_youtube_script_topic-name.md`

## Retention Rules (Data-Backed)

### 1. Always include a "what NOT to do" section
- Retention data shows viewers re-engage when told what to avoid (+2.5 pts at the 62-69% mark)
- Place in the back half (after delivering value, before CTA)
- Builds trust through honesty

### 2. Use interactive/animated data visuals, not static slides
- Animated visuals caused +2.0 pt retention bumps in testing
- Static slides and talking head alone bleed retention
- Every data-heavy section should have an animated visual

### 3. Max 2-3 deep examples in any list format, then fast montage
- Sequential item-by-item analysis loses viewers after the 2nd-3rd item
- Structure: 2-3 deep dives with concrete examples, then fast montage for the rest
- Deep examples need specific numbers
- Fast montage = 3-4 seconds per item, dynamic cuts

### 4. Result-first structure
- Show the outcome/result before explaining the process
- Front-loading theory before tutorials causes retention drops
- Result-first improved retention from 25.6% to 32.4%

### 5. Sub-hooks between sections
- Use mini cliffhangers between sections
- Each section ending should tease the next section's value

## Storytelling Rules

### 1. BUT/THEREFORE, Never "And Then"
- South Park rule: replace "and then" with "but" or "therefore"
- Creates cause-effect chains instead of flat sequences

### 2. Zoom Into the Moment
- Don't summarize - enter the scene with details
- Include location, actions, raw thoughts, dialogue

### 3. Show Don't Tell (Emotions)
- "I was disappointed" BAD - "I leaned back and took a long breath" GOOD

### 4. Rhythm (Gary Provost Rule)
- Mix short, medium, and long sentences
- Same-length sentences = monotone = viewer drops

### 5. Conversational Tone
- Write as if sending a voice message to a friend

### 6. Stepping Stones
- Each info piece must logically connect to the next
- Never skip steps

### 7. Information Flow (Dual Thread)
- Give 2 things at once: what's happening now + what's coming (teaser)

### 8. Direction (End First)
- Write the last sentence of each chapter first, then work backwards
- Last sentence must be shareable on its own

### 9. Dialogue Over Summary
- "My friend was surprised" BAD - "My friend said: 'Wait, did you do this?'" GOOD

## Quality Bar
- Scripts must have a clear hook in the first 30 seconds
- Every script must include at least one CTA
- Every script must include a "what NOT to do" section
- No filler - every section must earn its place
- List-format videos: max 3 deep dives before switching to fast montage
- Match the brand voice throughout
